The Lot

Home page

Recommended times to skate:

  • 6:00 AM to 8:00 AM
  • 5:30 PM to 11:30 PM

    • The lot holds a very special place in my heart because it is the place I consider to have first became comfortable riding a skateboard. Lot 51, as known to Yale administration and security, takes up almost the entirety of the space between Temple and college street. This massive amount of space combined with its incredibly convenient proximity to central campus(right across from cross-campus) makes it the perfect spot for the novice skater to get comfortable on the board while also allowing the more experienced skater to work on their fundamentals and still have little fun. Here are some of my favorite features of the lot and included with it are suggestions for what tricks and skills I think go well with them.

    General tips


  • Don’t be too loud if you’re skating outside of the recommended times because one of the buildings surrounding the lot is part of the school of music and they’re not too fond of skaters
  • There is no grocery store in close proximity so make sure to pack some water and/or snacks before a long sesh
  • Some people might still call Yale security, but they’re usually very understanding and will only tell you to leave the lot for the day

    • Favorite Features

    The Crack

    The crack’s purpose is just in its name. It is a small crack that runs along the center of the lot in between two parking spots. The crack is perfect for working on any trick that doesn’t require movement. Anything from Ollies to practicing stationary manual are perfect on the crack because it braces your back wheels just enough that the board doesn’t move without keeping too strong of a grip on it.

    Cones and Boards

    The spaciousness of the lot also allows for the ability to create your own obstacles and structures. You can find long wooden boards close to the lot thanks to the construction going on behind slifka. You can use this to practice your Ollies and also rolling off curbs. Just make sure to put it back! You will also find random cones scattered around the lot to create structures of whatever height to practice your Ollies and hippy jumps.

    Hendrie Curb

    The hendrie curb serves a similar purpose to the slick curb, but it is slightly smaller and has a much shorter run up. Although the shortness of the platforms length makes building up speed difficult, there is a ramp connected to it that allows you to build up speed for any trick off the curb. The height of the curbs also unlocks the ability to start working on balance and grinding by working on sloppy grinds and similar tricks.

    Slifka Curb

    The slifka curb is your typical sidewalk curve which is what makes it so perfect. It is the the most practical portion of the park because you can learn to get comfortable with getting over curb warning pavers, skating off curbs and also ollying onto curbs.

    The Ramp

    The ramp is a smooth and long incline in front of Hendrie Hall with a wide curb at the end. Ollying over the curve is great way to learn to clear ground obstacles because the ramp allows you to build up enough speed. My only caution is that there is a wooden fence next to it so be wary of your arms when clearing the curb. Outside of working on balance without pushing off, you can also practice your tic tacs and carving by trying to turn into the hendrie curb or riding off the bottom of the ramp.

    The Ramp

    I would only recommend trying the 4 stair if you have a couple of months of practice and are really comfortable with your Ollies. The stairs are long and there is decently tall which also makes them perfect for firecrackering down them. The stairs also have rails for those that really want to tempt fate.
